1. Crisis – the collapse of 48V at AI scale

As AI and HPC racks push beyond 30 kW into the 50–100 kW range, traditional 48V power architectures run into hard physical limits: extreme current, brutal I²R losses, massive copper and untenable cable spaghetti. Thermal headroom disappears and the business model for brownfield upgrades starts to break.

危機：隨著 AI／HPC 機櫃功率密度爆炸式突破 30–100 kW，傳統 48V 架構在電流、損耗與散熱上全面失衡， 銅耗與佈線成本失控，已無法支撐下一代算力需求。

1.1 48V under stress

  • Rack power jumps from 10 kW to 30–100 kW, but legacy 48V busways were never designed for kilo-amp currents.
  • I²R loss explodes: doubling current quadruples power loss. Copper cross-section grows, cost and weight follow.
  • Cable-PDU “spaghetti” blocks airflow, complicates maintenance and turns brownfield upgrades into risky art projects.

1.2 Why 800V HVDC?

For the same power P = V·I, raising voltage from 48V to 800V (≈16×) cuts current by 16×. Since Ploss = I²·R, distribution loss drops by 16² ≈ 256×. This is the real driver behind 800V HVDC at the rack-cluster level.

  • Slimmer conductors, smaller busbars, more practical cable management.
  • Huge reduction in copper cost and weight per MW deployed.
  • Better end-to-end efficiency from grid interface all the way to the GPU.

2. Physics – I²R, materials and HVDC safety

Three pillars define safe HVDC infrastructure: loss physics (I²R), materials under stress (hydrogen embrittlement, tin whiskers) and creepage/clearance in high-voltage busbars and connectors.

2D comparison: 48V vs 800V for 100 kW load

Assuming identical conductor resistance R and power P = 100 kW: at 48V, current ≈ 2083 A; at 800V, current ≈ 125 A. I²R loss at 48V is ~ (2083/125)² ≈ 278× higher than at 800V.

2.1 Hydrogen embrittlement – C10100 vs C11000

In HVDC environments, oxygen-free high-conductivity copper (C10100) is preferred over C11000. C11000 may release water vapor under certain conditions, driving micro-cracks and hydrogen embrittlement in stressed regions. Over time this can lead to structural failure at busbar joints and grounding interfaces.

2.2 Tin whiskers – why silver plating is mandatory

Pure tin plating can grow microscopic conductive whiskers. At 12V or 48V they are annoying; at 800V HVDC, a single whisker can bridge critical creepage distance and trigger catastrophic arc-flash events. This is why modern HVDC busbars and connectors mandate silver plating or non-tin finishes.

3. Solution – structural, electrical and thermal co-design

For 100 kW+ racks, the bottleneck is no longer just power – it is cooling. Air alone cannot carry that much heat in a reasonable delta-T and airflow. Liquid cooling becomes mandatory, and the rack must be co-designed across mechanics, power and fluid circuits.

DLC (Direct-to-Chip)

Coolant flows through cold plates directly attached to CPUs/GPUs. Highest heat-flux efficiency, but requires precise manifolds, quick-disconnects and structural reinforcement of the rack to handle blind-mate insertion forces.

RDHx (Rear Door Heat Exchanger)

A large liquid-cooled radiator mounted on the rack rear door. Server fans push hot air through the heat exchanger, rejecting heat into the liquid loop. Ideal as a room-level upgrade path for medium/high density racks.

Immersion Cooling

Entire servers are submerged in dielectric fluid, either single-phase (no boiling) or two-phase (boiling for higher efficiency). Fantastic thermal performance but with challenges in fluid cost, maintenance and facility integration.

CDU (Coolant Distribution Unit)

The “heart” of the liquid loop. CDUs couple facility chilled water with rack-level coolant, regulate temperature/pressure/flow and provide instrumentation required for predictive maintenance and autonomous optimization.

4. Core products – ORV3, ORW and HVDC sidecar

Open Rack V3 (ORV3)

Latest OCP rack standard using 21-inch equipment width, native support for 48V power distribution and liquid cooling manifolds. ORV3 also introduces 48 mm OpenU (OU) height, enabling larger heatsinks and more efficient fans versus legacy 44.45 mm RU.

48V-LC Upgrade Kit

A complete upgrade bundle for existing ORV3 racks, including manifolds, CDU interfaces and cold plates. Designed for smooth transition from air-cooled 48V racks to hybrid air + liquid deployments without full rack replacement.

Open Rack Wide (ORW)

A widened rack for ultra-dense AI/HPC, accommodating more space for cabling, power and liquid manifolds. ORW is designed for 100 kW+ loads and aligns with emerging MW-scale AI rack designs (e.g. double-wide frames with crane-lift top points).

HVDC Power Sidecar

A dedicated cabinet hosting ±400 VDC (Diablo 400) or 800 VDC distribution units with full safety features: interlock doors, intrusion switches, ground-fault detection and provision for high-impedance grounding schemes.

8. Technical FAQ

Q1: Why transition from 48V to 800V HVDC?

Because of current and efficiency. At 100 kW, 48V needs >2000 A – impossible to handle without massive copper and extreme I²R heating. At 800V, current drops to ~125 A, losses are reduced by >99.7%, cables become manageable and PDU-to-chip efficiency improves dramatically.

Q2: What structural requirements does liquid cooling impose on ORV3 racks?

Liquid cooling adds hundreds of kilograms of static load and significant insertion forces from blind-mate quick-disconnects. ORV3 liquid cooling specs therefore call for web stiffeners and reinforced posts to prevent long-term frame deformation.

Q3: Do I need a new “greenfield” data center to adopt OCP?

No. OCP solutions can work in brownfield facilities. Products like 21→19-inch adapters, 48V-LC upgrade kits and OCP Ready™ facility assessments exist precisely to enable incremental adoption without throwing away existing investments.
